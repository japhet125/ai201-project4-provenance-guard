# Provenance Guard Planning Document

## Project Purpose

Provenance Guard is a backend system for creative writing platforms. Its purpose is to analyze submitted text, estimate whether it was likely written by a human or generated by AI, communicate uncertainty clearly, and give creators a way to appeal classifications they believe are wrong.

The goal is not to perfectly detect AI writing. Perfect AI detection is not realistic. The goal is to build a transparent, multi-signal system that avoids overconfidence, especially because falsely labeling human writing as AI-generated can harm creators.

---

## Architecture

### Submission Flow

```text
User submits text
      |
      v
POST /submit
      |
      v
Validate JSON input
(text + creator_id)
      |
      v
Detection Pipeline
      |
      |--> Signal 1: Groq LLM classification
      |
      |--> Signal 2: Stylometric heuristics
      |
      v
Confidence scoring
      |
      v
Transparency label generation
      |
      v
Structured audit log
      |
      v
JSON response to user
```

### Appeal Flow

```text
Creator contests classification
      |
      v
POST /appeal
      |
      v
Validate content_id and creator_reasoning
      |
      v
Update content status to "under_review"
      |
      v
Append appeal to audit log
      |
      v
Return appeal confirmation
```

A submitted piece of text first enters the system through `POST /submit`. The system validates the request, sends the text through multiple detection signals, combines those signals into a confidence score, maps that score to a transparency label, stores the decision in the audit log, and returns a structured JSON response.

If a creator disagrees with the result, they can submit an appeal through `POST /appeal`. The appeal records the creator's reasoning, updates the content status to `under_review`, and appends the appeal information to the audit log.

Response body:

    "stylometric_score": 0.77

Allows a creator to contest a classification.

Request body:

Response body:

```json

  "content_id": "unique-content-id",
  "status": "under_review",
  "message": "Appeal received and marked for review."

```

## Detection Signals

### Signal 1: Groq LLM Classification

This signal uses a Groq-hosted language model to review the submitted text and estimate whether it appears more likely AI-generated or human-written.

What it measures:

* Overall writing style
* Semantic coherence
* Generic phrasing
* Overly polished or formulaic language
* Whether the text resembles common AI-generated patterns

Output:

```json
{
  "score": 0.0 to 1.0,
  "reason": "Short explanation of the LLM judgment."
}
```

A score closer to `1.0` means the text appears more AI-generated. A score closer to `0.0` means the text appears more human-written.

Why this signal is useful:

The LLM can detect broad stylistic patterns that are difficult to capture with simple statistics.

Blind spot:

The LLM may be biased against formal writing, non-native English writing, or highly edited human writing. It may also miss AI-generated text that has been heavily revised by a human.

---

### Signal 2: Stylometric Heuristics

This signal uses pure Python calculations to measure structural features of the text.

Metrics:

* Average sentence length
* Sentence length variance
* Type-token ratio, meaning vocabulary diversity
* Punctuation density

What it measures:

Stylometry measures the structure of writing rather than the meaning. AI-generated text often has smoother sentence lengths, more uniform structure, and moderate vocabulary diversity. Human writing often has more irregularity, stronger bursts, fragments, repetition, and uneven punctuation.

Output:

```json
{
  "score": 0.0 to 1.0,
  "metrics": {
    "avg_sentence_length": 18.5,
    "sentence_length_variance": 4.2,
    "type_token_ratio": 0.62,
    "punctuation_density": 0.08
  }
}
```

A score closer to `1.0` means the structure appears more AI-like. A score closer to `0.0` means the structure appears more human-like.

Why this signal is useful:

It is independent from the LLM signal. It does not ask a model to judge the text; it computes measurable writing features.

Blind spot:

Short texts, poems, formal essays, and edited human writing may produce misleading stylometric scores.

## Confidence Scoring

The system combines both signals into a single AI-likelihood score.

Initial weighting:

```text
Combined score = (0.60 * LLM score) + (0.40 * stylometric score)
```

Reasoning:

The LLM signal gets slightly more weight because it can evaluate meaning and style holistically. The stylometric signal still receives significant weight because it is independent and measurable.

The confidence score represents how strongly the system believes the content appears AI-generated.

Important interpretation:

* `0.50` means the system is unsure.
* A score near `1.00` means strong evidence of AI-generated writing.
* A score near `0.00` means strong evidence of human writing.
* Scores near the middle should not be treated as confident classifications.

Because a false positive is harmful, the system uses conservative thresholds before labeling content as likely AI-generated.

Thresholds:

| Combined Score | Attribution  | Label Type            |
| -------------- | ------------ | --------------------- |
| 0.00–0.35      | likely_human | High-confidence human |
| 0.36–0.69      | uncertain    | Uncertain             |
| 0.70–1.00      | likely_ai    | High-confidence AI    |

This approach avoids accusing writers of using AI when the system has only weak or mixed evidence.

## Transparency Label Design

The transparency label is the plain-language text shown to readers.

### High-confidence AI label

> "Provenance Guard found strong signals that this content may have been AI-generated. This label is based on automated analysis and may be appealed by the creator."

### High-confidence human label

> "Provenance Guard found strong signals that this content was likely written by a human. This label is based on automated analysis and should not be treated as absolute proof."

### Uncertain label

> "Provenance Guard could not confidently determine whether this content was written by a human or generated by AI. Readers should interpret this result as uncertain."

These labels are designed to be clear, cautious, and fair. They avoid claiming certainty because AI detection is imperfect.

## Appeals Workflow

A creator can submit an appeal if they believe their content was misclassified.

Who can appeal:

Any creator with a valid `content_id`.

Required appeal information:

* `content_id`
* `creator_reasoning`

System behavior after appeal:

1. Find the original content decision in the audit log.
2. Add the creator's appeal reasoning.
3. Update the content status to `under_review`.
4. Append an appeal event to the audit log.
5. Return a confirmation message.

A human reviewer would see:

* Original submitted text
* Original attribution result
* Confidence score
* Individual signal scores
* Transparency label
* Creator appeal reasoning
* Current status: `under_review`

Automated re-classification is not required. The goal is to provide a fair review path.

## Rate Limiting Plan

The submission endpoint will use Flask-Limiter.

Chosen limit:

```text
10 submissions per minute
100 submissions per day
```

Reasoning:

A normal creator on a writing platform is unlikely to submit more than 10 pieces of content in one minute. The per-minute limit protects the system from spam or scripted abuse. The daily limit allows regular use while preventing one user or bot from flooding the API.

The appeal endpoint can use a stricter limit later, such as 5 appeals per minute, because appeals should be less frequent than normal submissions.

## Anticipated Edge Cases

### Edge Case 1: Formal human writing

A human-written academic essay or professional blog post may appear polished and structured. The LLM and stylometric signals may score it as AI-like even though it is human-written.

Mitigation:

Use conservative AI thresholds and route medium-confidence cases to the uncertain label.

### Edge Case 2: Poetry or experimental writing

Poetry may use repetition, short lines, unusual punctuation, and simple vocabulary. Stylometric heuristics may misread this as AI-like or structurally abnormal.

Mitigation:

Document this limitation clearly and avoid overconfident labels for unusual writing styles.

### Edge Case 3: Lightly edited AI text

A creator may generate AI text and edit it enough to make it look more human. The system may produce an uncertain or human-leaning score.

Mitigation:

Use multiple signals and acknowledge that the system estimates likelihood, not proof.

### Edge Case 4: Non-native English writing

Non-native English writers may use repeated phrases, formal structure, or unusual sentence patterns. A detection system could unfairly score this as AI-like.

Mitigation:

Use careful label language and preserve the appeal workflow.

---

## False Positive Scenario

A false positive happens when a human writer's work is labeled as likely AI-generated.

This is the most harmful error because it can damage a creator's reputation. To reduce this risk, the system requires a combined score of at least `0.70` before using the high-confidence AI label.

If the score falls between `0.36` and `0.69`, the system returns the uncertain label instead of accusing the creator. If the creator still disagrees, they can submit an appeal. The appeal updates the status to `under_review` and records the creator's explanation in the audit log.
